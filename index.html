I

Guccio Gucci watched from a distance as wealthy tourists, graceful and otherworldly, touted beautiful luxury suitcases to and fro. Although they passed within mere inches of him, they still somehow seemed as if they existed lightyears away.


An Italian immigrant working as a hotel attendant at the prestigious Savoy Hotel in London, young Guccio bore witness each and every day to the beauty that such garments and accessories could possess--and each and every day, his desire to create such beauty himself grew a little bit stronger.

II

Inspired by the fineries he’d lusted after for so long, Guccio returned to his hometown of Florence, Italy, in 1902 to make his dream a reality. In 1921, his eponymous brand came to life; Gucci, a name now revered for its brilliance, sprung into humble beginnings.

III

Employing local Florentine artisans, reknowned for their craftsmanship, Guccio pieced together the brand’s image. As their fame grew, Guccio, along with his three sons, Aldo, Vasco, and Rodolfo, worked to expand their empire; new stores opened in Florence, as well as in Milan and Rome, throughout the early- to mid-20th century. City by city, year by year, people began to take notice of Gucci’s fine leather handbags, suitcases, and shoes.


During this time, many of Gucci’s most iconic creations came to exist: their brown and tan, diamond-patterned luggage; their handbags with bamboo handles; their beautifully-crafted leather loafers; and the green and red bands by which millions around the world still identify the brand to this day.

IV

Just after the conclusion of World War II, two of the Gucci sons, Aldo and Rodolfo, opened the family’s first store in New York City. As they networked with celebrities and socialites of the day, the American film stars of the 1950s also began vacationing more and more often in Florence, returning home with Gucci goods and establishing the brand as an international symbol of glamour and luxury.


Just fifteen days after the opening of the New York store, the family laid dear Guccio Gucci to rest at age 72. His eldest son, Aldo, rose to the head of the company and worked to fill his late father’s footsteps.

V

As the decades passed, so did Gucci prosper; the 50s, 60s and 70s saw the brand thrive. Stores opened in London, Paris, Tokyo, Hong Kong, and Beverly Hills; icons like First Lady Jacqueline Kennedy and Princess Grace of Monaco carry ; By 1985, the accessory that perhaps champions as the brand’s most iconic creation—the Gucci leather loafer—went up on display at the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York, cementing the brand’s name as one to be eternally remembered.

VI

However, fame rarely comes without a price; and as Gucci’s international reputation continued to grow, as did its problems. It began with Maurizio Gucci, son of Rodolfo Gucci and heir to the throne, who took over the company from his Uncle Aldo in 1984—only to surrender the remainder of the family’s already-halved shares to merchant bank InvestiCorp in 1985. After over 60 years of work, the family’s involvement in the brand came to an end.


Then, the unexpected. Standing in a doorway in Milan on one fateful day in March 1995, Maurizio Gucci met his untimely fate: gunned down by a hitman who his own wife hired to kill him.

VII

The mid-90s left Gucci in a dangerous place. Anxious investors on one side and a disappointed marketplace on the other, the decades-old fashion house desperately needed a rebranding and a hero.
